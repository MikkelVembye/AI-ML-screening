STUDY-ID: 44259155:

– Title: ‘The effects of implementing a curriculum-based service learning program incorporating a choral reading model’

– Abstract: ‘As the 21\(\rm\sp{st}\) century becomes a reality, the challenge for new approaches for revitalizing curriculum and instructional delivery becomes more vital. We are focused to realize the impact that the current negative climate, coupled with tight and dwindling funding, have had on our public educational system. With these thoughts in mind, this study examines the impact of a curriculum-based service learning program for middle school students. A Choral Reading model for reading and communications classes was implemented for eighth grade students with the purpose of examining its impact in the area of improved speech and oral communication skills. These are areas which appear to be lacking in today s youth. The study also investigated evidence of improved cognitive learning and greater interest in poetry and literature. Whether an intergenerational program produced positive change in attitudes, views, and relationships of young adolescents towards senior citizens was also explored. This research study provided findings that indicated service learning is a viable curricular method which increase and encourage community support of public schools. It also showed that integrated into the curriculum, the Choral Reading Model is a successful instructional intervention for teaching both poetry and public speaking skills in reading and communications classes. Service learning is a context-based curricular intervention and is global in nature. The methodology for evaluating service learning initiatives in the past has been primarily qualitative. In this study, seven different qualitative and quantitative research measures were used and all of the findings showed strongly significant results. Three quantitative measures yielded statistically significant positive change. The t-ratios for the Rating Scales for the Oral Reading of Literature indicated significant improvement in all six categories evaluated (intelligibility, vocal loudness, fluency, tempo, intonation, and interpretive presentation) as well as in overall ability. The statistical analysis of the Written Attitude Scales and The Measure of Knowledge in Aging yielded evidence of strong positive attitude change on the part of the students. Qualitative assessments included student journal writing, student interviews, and elite interviews. The process of open and axial coding of this data indicated strong positive change. The Checklist of Personal Gains served to act as a bridge to connect, amplify, and corroborate the data from the other six assessments by relating the constructs from the qualitative assessments to the scores from the quantitative tests using the items on the Checklist which received the most responses.’

It is clearly focused on service learning as a curricular intervention. The abstract describes the use of multiple assessment methods, including three quantitative measures with statistical analyses (t-ratios, rating scales, written attitude scales, measure of knowledge in aging), indicating that it is a quantitative evaluation study (with additional qualitative components). Therefore, all inclusion criteria are met: A) focus on service learning, B) participants are K-12 students in general education, and C) the report includes quantitative evaluation of the intervention.

STUDY-ID: 44260814:

– Title: ‘Positive peer group interventions: An alternative to individualized interventions for promoting prosocial behavior in potentially disaffected youth’

– Abstract: ‘Introduction: Most approaches to reducing the socially inappropriate behavior of adolescents target the individual rather than a group. Evidence suggests greater efficiency and long-lasting effects may be achieved when groups of peers work together to make meaningful contributions to their communities through service learning projects. In the systems-intervention program presented here, titled Positive Peer Group, interventions are implemented with the goal of developing enhanced skills, increasing personal responsibility, increasing students bonding to their school community, developing an ability to manage conflict responsibly, identifying with positive peers, and increasing social skill repertoires. Method: The subjects were 198 students attending 17 schools with matched controls in Ohio, USA. Prior to entry into the program students exhibited characteristics signaling a capacity for leadership whether as positive role models or as potential leaders of a disaffected group likely to engage in negative behavior patterns. Students experiencing the experimental condition were offered opportunities to work together in small, heterogeneous groups on projects that contributed to improving their school or solving a problem that they themselves recognized within their school or community. Results: Self-report measures taken pre- and post-intervention confirm statistically significant improvements in behavioral accountability, bonding to school, anger management, and the establishment of a psychological sense of school membership—all primary goals for this intervention. Discussion and Conclusion: These data strongly support the contention that increased affiliation with the life of the school established through service-learning enhances social skill development and may inoculate children from becoming further disaffected. (PsycINFO Database Record (c) 2016 APA, all rights reserved)’

Although they are described as potentially disaffected and selected for leadership potential, they remain part of the general school population, not a specialized clinical or special-education-only sample. C) The study reports a quantitative evaluation design: students in the experimental condition are compared to matched controls, with pre- and post-intervention self-report measures and statistically significant improvements reported. This meets the criterion for a quantitative evaluation study. Therefore the study should be included (decision = 1).
